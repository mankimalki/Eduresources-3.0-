The Complete Guide to A-Levels in the UK

Everything you need to know about A-Level qualifications - from subject choices to exam preparation and university applications.

Sarah Johnson January 15, 2025 8 min read
Home / Resources / A-Levels Guide

A-Levels (Advanced Level qualifications) are a crucial stepping stone in the UK education system, typically taken by students aged 16-18. They're the most common route to university and are highly valued by employers. In this comprehensive guide, we'll cover everything you need to know about A-Levels, from choosing subjects to exam preparation strategies.

What Are A-Levels?

A-Levels are subject-based qualifications that students in England, Wales and Northern Ireland typically study between ages 16-18 (after completing GCSEs). They're the most common route to university and are also valued by employers.

Key facts about A-Levels:

  • Usually studied over two years (AS Level in Year 1 and A2 Level in Year 2)
  • Students typically take 3-4 subjects
  • Graded from A* (highest) to E (minimum pass)
  • Assessed through exams and sometimes coursework
  • Offered by several exam boards including AQA, Edexcel and OCR

Did you know? The A* grade was introduced in 2010 to help universities distinguish between the very top students. Only about 8-10% of students achieve A* grades in their A-Levels.

Choosing Your A-Level Subjects

Selecting the right A-Level subjects is crucial as it can affect your university options and future career paths. Here are some key considerations:

1. University Requirements

If you have a particular degree in mind, check if it has specific subject requirements. For example:

  • Medicine: Chemistry is usually essential, plus Biology and one other science/mathematics
  • Engineering: Mathematics and Physics are typically required
  • English Literature: English Literature A-Level is often expected

2. Facilitating Subjects

Some subjects are particularly valued by universities as they prepare you well for degree-level study. These "facilitating subjects" include:

  • Mathematics and Further Mathematics
  • English Literature
  • Physics, Chemistry, Biology
  • Geography
  • History
  • Modern and Classical Languages

3. Subject Combinations

A good mix of subjects can keep your options open. Common combinations include:

Career Path Recommended Subjects
Medicine/Healthcare Chemistry, Biology, Mathematics/Physics
Engineering Mathematics, Physics, Further Mathematics/Chemistry
Law English Literature, History, a language
Business/Economics Mathematics, Economics, Business Studies
Arts/Humanities English Literature, History, a language

Pro Tip: If you're unsure about your future path, choosing two facilitating subjects plus one you enjoy can keep your options open while ensuring you study something you're passionate about.

A-Level Grading System

A-Levels are graded from A* (the highest) to E (the minimum pass). The grade boundaries vary by subject and exam board but typically:

  • A*: 90%+ overall (with 90%+ in A2 units)
  • A: 80-89%
  • B: 70-79%
  • C: 60-69%
  • D: 50-59%
  • E: 40-49% (minimum passing grade)

Ungraded (U) means you've failed to meet the minimum requirements.

UCAS Tariff Points

Universities often use the UCAS Tariff to compare different qualifications. Here's how A-Level grades convert:

A-Level Grade UCAS Points
A* 56
A 48
B 40
C 32
D 24

How to Prepare for A-Level Exams

Effective preparation is key to A-Level success. Here are our top strategies:

1. Create a Revision Timetable

Start early (at least 3 months before exams) and create a realistic schedule that covers all subjects.

2. Use Active Revision Techniques

  • Past papers: The single most effective revision tool
  • Mind maps: Great for visual learners
  • Flashcards: Ideal for memorizing key facts
  • Teaching others: Helps consolidate your understanding

3. Understand the Mark Schemes

Exam technique is crucial. Practice with mark schemes to understand what examiners are looking for.

4. Focus on Weak Areas

Use your mock exam results to identify and prioritize weaker topics.

5. Look After Yourself

Regular breaks, exercise, good nutrition and sleep are essential for optimal performance.

Revision Tip: The Pomodoro technique (25 minutes focused study followed by a 5-minute break) can significantly improve concentration and retention.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many A-Levels should I take?

Most students take 3 A-Levels, although some take 4 if they're particularly able. Universities typically make offers based on 3 A-Levels.

Can I change my A-Level subjects?

Most schools allow changes in the first few weeks. After that, you'll likely need to catch up on missed work.

What's the difference between AS and A2?

AS Level is the first year of study (standalone qualification), while A2 is the second year that completes the full A-Level.

How many hours should I study outside class?

Aim for 4-5 hours per subject per week, increasing as exams approach.

What if I fail an A-Level?

You can usually retake exams in November or the following summer. Some schools offer one-year A-Level retake courses.
